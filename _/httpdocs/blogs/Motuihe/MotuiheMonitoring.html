Motuihe Monitoring

Saturday March 10th 2012

The Motuihe Island Restoration Trust volunteers restoring Motuihe Island are also monitoring bird, plant, insect and reptile populations. This monitoring has enabled the Trust to collect baseline data of the plants and animals on the island early in it’s progress from poor quality farm land to native bush. There are plans in place to regularly re-sample all this data to enable the tracking of progress and the changing mix of species as the island is reforested. (See the summary of the report)

The volunteers of The Trust are restoring Motuihe Island, in Auckland’s Hauraki Gulf. They have launched into an ambitious program of pest control, weed control, reforestation (to the tune of 50,000 trees planted a year), and reintroduction of native wildlife. Working with a host of partners, Trust volunteers have reintroduced endangered native birds including kiwi, saddleback, bellbird and kakariki.

To enable accurate and consistent monitoring, 40 monitoring sites have been set up over the island. The sites have been placed in a mixture of environments, swamp, pasture, bush and beach. At each of these GPS’ed monitoring stations there is:

  • One bird observation point, for 5 minute bird counts.
  • Two vegetation monitoring points, separated by 40 metres, to catalogue the size and species of all vegetation found.
  • One reptile habitat, to track the change in skink and lizard populations.
  • Two invertebrate monitoring site, separated by 20 metres, to catalog the different insect species found

While carrying out this monitoring as a volunteer for the Trust I thought it would also be a good way to establish a visual record of the change occurring on the island. I started taking pictures at every monitoring station I went to, all the pictures were taken facing north at the same zoom level (18mm with 1.5 crop ratio giving 27mm as 35mm equivalent, for those that worry about such things). On a recent visit (November 2011) I managed to get round all 40 sites in one weekend, again taking pictures at each site.

So now with most sites having 2 or 3 pictures I can start to share the progress the Island has made, the map above has each monitoring spot marked on it, clicking on these spots will bring up the photos taken at these spots, if you move your mouse over the photos you will get a bigger version of the photo.
